Chapter 1: Semiconductors for Photodetection

1.1 Silicon — The Starting Point

Silicon is the workhorse of electronics and the first semiconductor used for photodetection. Its key properties:

PropertyValueImplication
Bandgap $E_g$1.12 eVCutoff wavelength $\lambda_c = 1240/1.12 \approx 1100$ nm
Crystal structureDiamond cubicMature, cheap fabrication
Native oxide$\text{SiO}_2$ (excellent)Enables MOS technology
Electron mobility1350 cm²/V·sModerate speed
Limitation: Silicon is opaque above ~1100 nm. The telecom wavelengths (1310 nm, 1550 nm) used in fiber-optic communication are far beyond silicon's absorption edge. For near-infrared detection, we need a different material.

1.2 Why III-V Semiconductors?

III-V compounds (elements from groups III and V of the periodic table) offer:

  • Tunable bandgap — by alloying GaAs with InAs, we can cover 0.35–2.5 eV
  • Direct bandgap — strong optical absorption (unlike silicon's indirect gap)
  • High electron mobility — faster carrier transport
  • Lattice matching — certain compositions share the same crystal lattice constant, enabling epitaxial growth of heterostructures

1.3 InGaAs and InP — The Telecom Materials

InGaAs (In$_{0.53}$Ga$_{0.47}$As)

$$E_g = 0.75 \text{ eV} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda_c = \frac{1240}{0.75} \approx 1650 \text{ nm}$$
  • Absorbs strongly from 1000–1700 nm — covers both telecom windows (1310 nm and 1550 nm)
  • High absorption coefficient: $\alpha \sim 10^4$ cm$^{-1}$ — a 1.5 μm layer absorbs >95% of 1310 nm light
  • Direct bandgap → efficient photogeneration

InP (Indium Phosphide)

$$E_g = 1.35 \text{ eV} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \lambda_c \approx 920 \text{ nm}$$
  • Transparent at 1310/1550 nm — used as the window and multiplication layer
  • Excellent breakdown properties — high ionization threshold, low noise
  • Lattice matched to InGaAs at $a = 5.8687$ Å — enables defect-free epitaxial growth
Key insight: The InGaAs/InP system lets us absorb at telecom wavelengths in InGaAs while multiplying in InP. This separation is the foundation of SACM SPADs.

Chapter 2: Avalanche Photodiode (APD) Theory

2.1 The I-V Characteristic

An APD is a p-n junction operated in reverse bias. The current has several regimes:

Figure 2: I-V characteristic showing dark current (blue) and illuminated current (orange) vs reverse bias.

  1. Low reverse bias ($V \ll V_{BR}$): Small dark current from thermal generation. Photocurrent is roughly constant (one electron-hole pair per absorbed photon).
  2. Mid reverse bias: Depletion region widens. Dark current increases slowly. Still no multiplication.
  3. Near breakdown ($V \to V_{BR}$): Impact ionization begins. Carriers gain enough energy to create secondary pairs. Current starts rising exponentially.
  4. At breakdown ($V = V_{BR}$): Gain $M \to \infty$. A single carrier triggers a self-sustaining avalanche. This is the Geiger mode used in SPADs.

2.2 Multiplication and Gain

When a carrier (electron or hole) travels through the high-field multiplication region, it can impact-ionize:

$$\alpha(E) = \text{ionization probability per cm (electrons)}$$ $$\beta(E) = \text{ionization probability per cm (holes)}$$

The multiplication factor $M$ is the average number of electron-hole pairs produced by one injected carrier. It diverges at breakdown:

$$M = \frac{1}{1 - (V/V_{BR})^n}$$

where $n$ depends on the material and device structure (typically 2–6).

Figure 3: Comprehensive I-V showing dark current, primary photocurrent, total illuminated current, and gain vs bias near breakdown.

2.3 Excess Noise Factor

Avalanche multiplication is a statistical process — the gain fluctuates from event to event. The excess noise factor $F(M)$ quantifies this:

$$F(M) = k_{\text{eff}} M + (1 - k_{\text{eff}})\left(2 - \frac{1}{M}\right)$$ $$k_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\beta}{\alpha} \quad \text{(ionization rate ratio)}$$
$k_{\text{eff}}$NoiseMaterial
$k = 1$Worst: $F = 2M - 1$Si (if both carriers ionized equally)
$k = 0$Best: $F = 2$Asymmetric ionization
$k \approx 0.4$ModerateInP (our material)

Figure 4: Excess noise factor $F(M)$ vs gain $M$ for different $k_{\text{eff}}$ values.

2.4 Quantum Efficiency

The external quantum efficiency (EQE) is the probability that an incident photon produces a measurable electron-hole pair:

$$\eta = (1 - R) \cdot \left(1 - e^{-\alpha_{\text{abs}} \cdot L_{\text{abs}}}\right) \cdot \eta_{\text{collection}}$$
  • $(1 - R)$: Surface reflection loss ($R \approx 0.3$ for uncoated InP)
  • $1 - e^{-\alpha L}$: Absorption probability in the InGaAs layer
  • $\eta_{\text{collection}}$: Probability that photo-generated carriers reach the multiplication region

The photodetection probability (PDP) adds the trigger probability:

$$\text{PDP}(\lambda) = \eta(\lambda) \cdot P_{\text{trigger}}$$

Chapter 3: Single-Photon Avalanche Diodes

3.1 Why Move from APD to SPAD?

FeatureAPD (Linear Mode)SPAD (Geiger Mode)
Bias$V < V_{BR}$$V > V_{BR}$ (overbias)
GainFinite ($M \sim 10$–$100$)Effectively infinite (self-sustaining)
OutputAnalog current $\propto$ optical powerDigital pulse (avalanche or no avalanche)
Sensitivity~nWSingle photon
TimingLimited by RC, transit timeSub-ns jitter possible
CircuitTransimpedance amplifierQuench circuit (active or passive)
Key advantage: A SPAD can detect a single photon. The avalanche gain is so high that one photo-carrier produces a measurable current pulse (μA–mA), detectable with simple electronics.

3.2 SPAD Architectures

SAM — Separate Absorption and Multiplication

  • Structure: InGaAs absorber + InP multiplier, no charge sheet
  • Problem: The electric field extends into the absorber, causing tunneling dark current
  • Use: Simple but limited performance

SAGCM — Separate Absorption, Grading, Charge, Multiplication

  • Structure: Adds a grading layer (InGaAsP) and a charge sheet (n+ InP)
  • Grading layer: Smooths the valence band discontinuity at InP/InGaAs interface — prevents carrier trapping
  • Charge sheet: Controls the field distribution — high field in InP (for multiplication), low field in InGaAs (reduces tunneling)
  • This is our architecture

SACM — Separate Absorption, Charge, Multiplication

  • Similar to SAGCM but without the grading layer
  • Valence band barrier causes hole trapping → afterpulsing

Reach-Through

  • Depletion region extends from multiplication all the way through absorber to contact
  • Good collection efficiency but harder to control field profile

Chapter 4: Physics Models in the Simulator

4.1 Poisson Solver

Solves for electrostatic potential $\phi(x)$ and field $E(x)$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[\varepsilon(x)\frac{d\phi}{dx}\right] = -q\left[p(x) - n(x) + N_D(x) - N_A(x)\right]$$

Carrier densities via Boltzmann statistics: $n = n_i e^{(\phi-\phi_n)/V_T}$, $p = n_i e^{(\phi_p-\phi)/V_T}$.

Solved with Newton-Raphson iteration on a 500-node uniform grid. Voltage ramped from 0 to $V_{\text{bias}}$ in 2V steps for convergence.

Figure 5: Electrostatic potential $\phi(x)$ at different reverse biases (0V to $V_{BR}+5$V).

4.2 Impact Ionization

Three models available (Okuto-Crowell is default):

Okuto-Crowell

$$\alpha(E,T) = \frac{E}{E_{th}} \exp\!\left\{A - \sqrt{A^2 + \left(\frac{E_{th}}{E\lambda}\right)^2}\right\}, \quad A = 0.217\left(\frac{E_{th}}{E_R}\right)^{1.14}$$

Temperature dependence via $\lambda(T) = \lambda_0 \tanh(\hbar\omega/2k_BT)$.

Chynoweth (simplest)

$$\alpha(E) = A \cdot e^{-B/E}, \quad B = E_{th}/\lambda$$

Wolff (semi-physical)

$$\alpha(E) = \frac{E}{E_{th}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\left(\frac{E_{th}}{E\lambda}\right)^2\right)$$

4.3 Trigger Probability (McIntyre)

Given $\alpha(x)$ and $\beta(x)$, the trigger probability for electron injection at $x$:

$$\mu(x) = \exp\!\left(\int_x^W [\alpha - \beta]\,ds\right), \quad M(x) = \frac{\mu(x)}{1 - \int_x^W \beta\mu\,ds}, \quad P_e(x) = 1 - \frac{1}{M(x)}$$

What are $\mu$ and $M$?

SymbolMeaning
$\mu(x)$Net multiplication integral — captures the asymmetry between electron and hole ionization. When $\alpha > \beta$, electrons multiply faster; $\mu$ quantifies this directional bias.
$M(x)$Mean avalanche gain — average number of e-h pairs produced by one carrier at $x$. Diverges at breakdown when the positive-feedback denominator $\to 0$.

Figure 6: Electron trigger probability $P_e(x)$ and hole trigger probability $P_h(x)$ at different excess biases.

4.4 Dark Current Components

Three physical mechanisms contribute to dark current:

ComponentFormulaPhysics
SRH$J = q \cdot n_i / (2\tau)$Thermal generation via mid-gap traps in depletion region
BTBT$J = A F^2 e^{-B E_g^{3/2}/F}$Band-to-band tunneling at high field
TATHurkx modelTrap-assisted tunneling — intermediate between SRH and BTBT

DCR (dark count rate) only counts carriers that trigger an avalanche:

$$\text{DCR} = A_{\text{det}} \int G_{\text{dark}}(x) \cdot P_{\text{trigger}}(x)\, dx$$

4.5 Photon Detection Probability

PDP combines absorption, dead-zone transmission, and trigger probability:

$$\text{PDP}(\lambda) = (1-R) \cdot T_{\text{dead}}(\lambda) \int_{\text{absorber}} \alpha(\lambda)\, e^{-\alpha(x-x_0)} \cdot P_{\text{trigger}}(x)\, dx$$

Figure 9: PDP vs wavelength at different excess biases. Peak PDP occurs where absorption and trigger probability are both high.

Figure 10: PDP at key wavelengths (1100, 1310, 1550, 1610 nm) vs excess bias.

4.6 Carrier Transport

Drift-diffusion with velocity saturation:

$$v_d = \frac{\mu E}{1 + |E|/E_{\text{sat}}}, \quad D = \mu V_T \left(1 + 2\left(\frac{|E|}{v_{\text{sat}}}\right)^2\right)$$

Position update: $x_{\text{new}} = x + v_d\,dt + \sqrt{2D\,dt}\cdot\xi$ (stochastic Wiener process).

4.7 Monte Carlo Avalanche

Simulates individual carrier histories:

  1. Inject seed carrier at $x_0$
  2. Drift-diffuse each carrier
  3. After dead space: sample ionization with $P_{\text{ion}} = 1 - e^{-\alpha \cdot dx}$
  4. On ionization: spawn new e-h pair
  5. Stop when carrier count $\geq N_{\text{threshold}}$

This captures the stochastic nature of avalanche build-up and gives timing jitter.
